## SECTION 1: THE VOICE, FULLY DEFINED

The Midnight Visitor Log host is a specific human being, not a content category.

**He is:**
- 45, from the Midwest, which means his default register is flat, plain, and sincere — not folksy, not performative
- The guy who owns every Troma film on physical media and can explain why *The Toxic Avenger* is actually a well-constructed narrative about class power
- Joe Bob Briggs without the cowboy hat but with all of the encyclopedic passion
- Fox Mulder if Mulder grew up in Iowa and got into the blues instead of the FBI
- Someone who facts-checks the thing he wants to believe, and still wants to believe it

**He knows:**
- This is a show. He's the host of a show. He's having the time of his life.
- The audience is in on it. This is not a trick or a lure — it's an invitation.
- The facts are real. The mystery is real. The frame is theater. All three things are true at once.

**He never:**
- Pretends to be scared of anything
- Does the spooky voice
- Mocks a witness
- Tells the audience what to conclude
- Takes himself seriously enough that he can't laugh at his own bit
- Loses the facts in the entertainment or loses the entertainment in the facts

## SECTION 4: EPISODE-ARCHITECTURE PHRASES

These are the internal grammar of the show — recurring structural phrases that create familiarity across episodes.

---

### THE WITNESS SIGNPOST

> **"One more person saw something they couldn't name."**

*Introduce each witness account in Act II with this line. After the third repetition, the audience has internalized the rhythm. The repetition is the point — it's a liturgy.*

---

### THE THEORY HANDOFF

> **"Here's what [the scientists / the skeptics / the official record] say. And here's the thing they don't say."**

*The host's pivot into each theory. Grants the skeptical position full respect before opening the door wider.*

---

### THE PERMISSION LINE

> **"I'm not here to tell you what to think. I'm here to show you what happened and let you take it home."**

*The show's philosophical commitment, stated plainly. Use once per episode, in the Theory section or at the Act III open.*

---

### THE GRAVITY MARKER

*For anchor events — bridge collapses, disasters, deaths. No host commentary. Just the facts.*

> **"[Number]. [Date]. [Blunt statement of what happened]."**
> "Forty-six people. December 15th. Rush hour."

*The host goes quiet here. Let the facts carry the weight. This is where Joe Bob stops making jokes because the movie just got real.*

---

### THE PRODUCTIVE UNCERTAINTY CLOSE

> **"I don't know what [they saw / it was / it means]. But I know [one specific detail that defies easy explanation]."**

*The episode's epistemological landing point. Not a verdict. An open door. This is the sentence the audience takes home.*

---

### THE JOE BOB INTRO RIFF

*This is the moment where the host introduces the episode's subject with the same energy Joe Bob brings to the night's feature — here's what we're watching, here's why it matters, here's what makes it worth your time at midnight.*

> **"Tonight's log is filed from [LOCATION]. The year is [YEAR]. And somebody out there is about to have the worst/strangest/most inexplicable [Tuesday / November / ordinary evening] of their life. Pull up a chair."**

---

### THE LDI LORE BRIDGE (optional, when subject connects to realm)

> **"The Cathedral of Glitch has a standing rule: error is not failure. Error is the system speaking its own true name. Whatever [SUBJECT LOCATION] was trying to say in [YEAR] — it said it loudly. We're still decoding the message."**

*Use sparingly — once per season maximum. This is the moment where the show acknowledges the world it lives inside.*

## SECTION 5: THE FIVE-TRIGGER CATCHPHRASE ARCHITECTURE

Every great Midnight Visitor Log phrase activates at least three of these five triggers. Use this system to generate new catchphrases for any episode.

| # | Trigger | What It Does | Test Question |
|---|---------|--------------|---------------|
| **1** | **The Tension Pivot** | Juxtaposes a mundane fact with an unexplainable implication | Does this sentence feel okay until the second half? |
| **2** | **The Witness Transfer** | Puts the viewer in the witness's position without telling them what to feel | Can the viewer see themselves there? |
| **3** | **The Permission Line** | Explicitly withholds judgment, which paradoxically invites the viewer's belief | Does it say "you don't have to believe this" while making them want to? |
| **4** | **The Plain Drop** | A flat Midwestern statement that carries more weight than its word count | Would this land harder if said quietly? |
| **5** | **The Open Door** | Ends on a question or invitation, never a verdict | Does it leave room for the viewer's own conclusion? |

**Template — generate new catchphrases for any subject:**

```
TENSION PIVOT:
"[Mundane fact]. [Number that makes it real]. [Thing nobody can explain]."

WITNESS TRANSFER:
"You're [everyday activity]. It's [time]. And [the impossible thing happens]."

PERMISSION LINE:
"I'm not asking you to [believe X]. I'm asking you to sit with [specific detail]."

PLAIN DROP:
"[Number]. [Date]. [What happened in four words or fewer]."

OPEN DOOR:
"I don't know what [it was / they saw / it means].
But [one concrete detail that resists easy explanation]."
```

## SECTION 7: WHAT THIS VOICE NEVER DOES

**Brand boundary list — The Last Roadside Attraction does NOT:**

- Generic horror (jump scares, spooky music stings, dramatic pauses for effect)
- Conspiracy slop (unsourced claims presented as fact, "they don't want you to know this")
- Mocking witnesses (every witness is a real person who reported something real — they get respect)
- Telling the audience what to believe (the permission structure is sacred)
- Overwrought self-seriousness (the host knows he's doing a show at midnight about cryptids and loves it)
- Ironic detachment (the opposite failure mode — this is not a snark vehicle)
- Unexplained lore drops (viewers who don't know the LDI realm should be able to enjoy every episode without a decoder ring)
